## 8. Limitations

### 8.1 Study Design Limitations

**Non-Experimental Design:**
- No control group (cannot isolate Calcora's effect from other study activities)
- Cannot claim causality (e.g., "Calcora improved exam scores")
- Self-selection bias (motivated students more likely to participate)

**Small Sample Size:**
- N=25 insufficient for statistical power on learning outcomes
- Pilot-scale appropriate for usability testing, not efficacy claims
- Not generalizable to broader student population

**Self-Reported Data:**
- Post-survey responses subject to social desirability bias
- Students may overestimate learning benefits
- No objective pre/post test of calculus skills

**Uncontrolled Variables:**
- Students used tool voluntarily (varying frequency and quality of engagement)
- Different instructors, courses, and institutional contexts
- Concurrent use of other tools (Wolfram Alpha, tutoring, etc.)

### 8.2 Technical Limitations

**Scope:**
- Only covers Calculus I/II single-variable techniques
- Cannot handle word problems requiring setup
- No support for multivariable, differential equations, linear algebra

**Interpretation:**
- Requires students to translate word problems into symbolic form
- Assumes students can interpret mathematical notation
- Limited help for conceptual understanding (focuses on procedures)

### 8.3 Generalizability

**Participant Characteristics:**
- All participants had reliable internet access (digital divide not addressed)
- Skewed toward STEM majors (may not represent business/econ calculus students)
- Self-selected participants may be more tech-savvy or motivated

**Institutional Context:**
- Pilot did not include students from [e.g., historically Black colleges, Hispanic-serving institutions, international contexts]
- Results may not generalize to different teaching styles (e.g., flipped classroom vs lecture)

## 9. Future Research

### 9.1 Recommended Next Steps

**Controlled Efficacy Study (Planned Fall 2026):**
- **Design:** Randomized controlled trial with N=100-200 students
- **Groups:** Calcora + instruction vs instruction only
- **Outcome Measures:** 
  - Pre/post test on procedural fluency
  - Exam performance comparison
  - Time-on-task efficiency
  - Math anxiety reduction (validated scale)
- **Duration:** Full semester (14-16 weeks)
- **Institutions:** 3-4 partner universities with IRB approval

**Longitudinal Study:**
- Track student retention and performance in subsequent courses (Calc II, Physics, Engineering)
- Does Calcora use in Calc I improve readiness for advanced topics?
- Follow cohort over 1-2 years

**Subgroup Analysis:**
- Which students benefit most? (Struggling vs high-achieving; first-time vs repeating calculus)
- Are there gender, race, or socioeconomic differences in effectiveness?
- How does prior math confidence moderate outcomes?

**Learning Analytics Research:**
- Analyze step-by-step viewing patterns (do students skip explanations?)
- Correlate usage frequency with exam performance
- Identify optimal usage patterns (how often? when in workflow?)

### 9.2 Open Research Questions

1. **Optimal Scaffolding:** What is the ideal verbosity level for different student populations?
2. **Dependency Question:** Does long-term use create reliance, or does it build independence?
3. **Transfer Effects:** Do students who use Calcora show better transfer to novel problem types?
4. **Metacognition:** Does error-checking with Calcora improve students' self-monitoring skills?
5. **Instructor Integration:** How can Calcora be optimally integrated into curriculum vs used ad-hoc?

### 10.3 Implications for Pedagogy

**For Instructors:**
- Calcora is a valuable supplement, not a replacement for instruction
- Best used as post-attempt verification tool (not as first resort)
- Can reduce office hours questions about routine computation
- Frees instructor time to focus on conceptual understanding and applications

**For Students:**
- Treat Calcora like a worked example textbook—study the reasoning, don't just copy
- Use after genuinely struggling first (5-10 minutes) to maximize learning
- Progress from Teacher mode → Detailed → Concise as skills develop
- Supplement with closed-book practice before exams

**For Institutions:**
- Consider adopting Calcora as recommended resource in syllabus
- Train TAs to integrate tool into help sessions
- Monitor academic integrity (ensure students submit own work)
- Support continued development and research

### 10.4 Final Remarks

This pilot study provides promising preliminary evidence that Calcora addresses a real need in calculus education: **accessible, transparent, and pedagogically-grounded computational support**. While the small sample size and observational design limit causal claims, the high usability ratings, positive qualitative feedback, and reported learning benefits warrant continued development and rigorous evaluation.
